Centralized Electrical

This year all our main electrical components are within one corner of our robot. This allows our wiring process to happen alongside the assembly of the robot. The robot had limited space for other components due to wanting most of the space for fuel, so centralizing the electrical system created a designated spot for all components without causing packaging problems.

DesignAll Main Components in One Corner
BenefitWiring Assembled Alongside Robot Build
AccessCentralized for Fast Pit Access
Space ConstraintMaximizes Internal Volume for Fuel
Point-to-Point Power Wires

We transitioned to a direct, point-to-point wiring philosophy to increase reliability and current efficiency. Early in the season, we struggled with short factory leads on swerve modules — our initial fix of using butt connectors to extend the wires proved to be a point of failure, as vibrations during testing led to wire pull-outs.

After consulting with team alumni, we moved to custom-fabricated power cables. By crimping ring terminals directly onto high-strand copper wire, we eliminated intermediate splices — resulting in a more secure mechanical connection to the PDH and a cleaner, snag-free wire run.

Previous ApproachButt Connectors for Extensions
SolutionCrimped Ring Terminals on High-Strand Copper Wire
BenefitNo Intermediate Splices, Secure PDH Connection

The Engineering Pivot

Even though our pneumatic system worked, we chose to remove it before our first competition. We realized the system was too heavy and complicated for what we actually needed on the field. By taking it off, we saved weight that we could use to make other parts of our robot stronger.

This taught us that sometimes the best engineering choice is knowing when to keep it simple.

DecisionRemoved Pneumatics Before Competition
ReasonToo Heavy and Complex for Benefit Provided
OutcomeWeight Savings Applied to Other Subsystems
CAN Bus Architecture

Last year we were able to implement a CANivore so we can have two different CAN networks. This lets us isolate our drivebase's communication from that of our manipulators — fixing the problem of high CAN bus utilization we had two years ago. Along with this, we have created a labeling system for CAN IDs across our different subsystems.

Dual Network Layout

Network 1Drivebase (CANivore) — 4× Drive Kraken x60, 4× Steer Kraken x44, Pigeon IMU
Network 2Manipulators (RIO CAN) — Intake, Spindexer, Indexer, Turret, Climber

Why Two Networks?

Isolation
Drivebase CAN traffic no longer competes with manipulator traffic. Swerve modules update faster and more reliably without high bus utilization degrading performance.
CAN ID Labeling
A consistent CAN ID labeling system is applied to all controllers, making it easy to identify devices at a glance during pit debugging and ensuring IDs never conflict between networks.
