Archetype Iterations
Three Robot Archetypes Considered · Inspired by 254, 118, and 4481 · Converged on Spindexer + Corner Turret

Before committing to a final robot architecture, we explored three distinct design archetypes. Each iteration informed the next, ultimately converging on the spindexer-fed corner turret design used on ATLAS.

Archetype Overview
V1
Rolldexer + Centered Turret
254 2017 "Misfire" inspired. Roller floor system feeding a rear-mounted shooter with a centered turret. Insufficient hopper volume for turret integration.
V2
Belt-Feed + Corner Shooter
118 2017 "Ruckus" inspired. Perpendicular belt transfer into a corner-mounted turret. Too complex — packaging and reliability concerns ruled it out.
V3
Spindexer + Corner Turret
Final design. 4481 "Era" inspired. ~100 Fuel capacity hopper with spindexer serialization into a corner-mounted turreted shooter. Simple, fast, reliable.
Iteration History
V1 — Rolldexer with Centered Turret
This iteration was heavily inspired by Team 254's 2017 robot, Misfire, particularly in the intake, indexing system, and expanding hopper architecture. The design utilized a roller-based floor system to translate Fuel toward the rear of the hopper and into the shooter, enabling continuous and controlled ball flow.

The primary deviation from 254's approach was the addition of a turreted shooter. However, as the design progressed, it became clear that sufficient space for an effective turret could not be achieved without significantly compromising hopper volume. Resolving these packaging constraints would have reduced ball capacity beyond our acceptable threshold.

Given these limitations, we determined that this architecture did not meet our performance requirements, and it was ultimately set aside as we returned to the drawing board.
V2 — 118 2017 Style Belt-Feed Indexer
To address our capacity constraints, we explored architectures that would allow us to relocate the shooter to a corner of the robot, improving internal volume utilization and enabling higher Fuel storage. This investigation led us to study Team 118's 2017 robot, Ruckus, which used a belt-driven system to transfer Fuel rearward into a secondary set of perpendicular belts that fed a corner-mounted turret.

We prototyped a similar concept at the block CAD level; however, the perpendicular belt transfer introduced significant complexity in packaging, alignment, and reliability. The added mechanical overhead did not align with our "simple, fast, reliable" philosophy, and the design was ultimately deemed more complicated than the performance gains justified. As a result, we chose to move on from this approach and return to the drawing board.
V3 — Spindexer into Corner Turret (Final)
Our final design converged on a spindexer-fed system integrated with a corner-mounted turret, paired with a high-capacity hopper capable of storing approximately 100 Fuel. This architecture allows for efficient indexing, controlled feeding, and continuous scoring while maintaining a compact and organized internal layout.

This solution reflects Team 1792's core design philosophy — simple, fast, and reliable — while still meeting the high-performance demands required for effective scoring output. The spindexer enables consistent ball management and minimizes jamming risk, while the turret provides flexible targeting without requiring full robot reorientation.

For this iteration, we drew significant inspiration from Team 4481, Team Rembrandts, specifically their 2026 robot Era, whose integration of indexing and turreted scoring informed many of our design decisions.
