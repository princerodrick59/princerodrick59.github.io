Our Improvements This Season

This year we improved our use of several tools and made strategic changes to our software stack based on lessons from the previous season.

AdvantageScope Expansion
We expanded our use of AdvantageScope, the logging tool developed by Team 6328 Mechanical Advantage. This included on-field replays, odometry improvements, and simulation testing — allowing us to debug issues and validate autonomous paths without requiring robot time.
PhotonVision → Limelight 4
After a PDSA cycle in the offseason, we switched our vision system from PhotonVision to Limelight (CTRE). Our previous season had a high rate of missed game pieces during autonomous and difficulty developing accurate paths. We use the Limelight 4 — the most recent model — for its high AprilTag performance, wide FOV, better energy usage, and more beginner-friendly documentation. We have found it significantly easier to set up and consistently accurate for global pose estimation.
PathPlanner → BLine
We switched from PathPlanner to BLine, a newly developed path planning system created by Team 2638 Rebel Robotics ("Introducing BLine: A New Rapid Polyline Autonomous Path Planning Suite" on Chief Delphi). BLine offers more computational efficiency, lower cross-track errors at waypoints, and less complicated controller tuning. In testing, the margin of error versus PathPlanner was significantly smaller — essential for consistent autonomous performance. We iterate on our autos continuously, adjusting robot speed and timing to minimize missed Fuel and balance cycle speed with consistency.
Autonomous Iteration

Over the season we iterated on our autonomous routines to make them more efficient. This involved testing different approaches to speed, lineup, and path consistency — then making deliberate decisions based on results.

Speed vs. Consistency Tradeoff
One key decision was whether to move slowly through the Neutral Zone to minimize scattered Fuel, or move swiftly to allow more cycles. We chose to prioritize the swift autonomous program to allow the chance for multiple cycles to the Neutral Zone.
Tracking Results
All autonomous testing results are tracked on the shop whiteboard first for accessibility, then transferred into our Autonomous documentation. This keeps the whole subteam informed on what has been tried and what the data shows.
Maintaining Standards

We continue building on the documentation and version control improvements we made last season to keep our code organized and knowledge accessible to all team members.

GitHub — Multi-Account Commits + Issues
We use a program to easily swap GitHub accounts in VS Code, ensuring commits are properly credited to the student who wrote them. We also use GitHub Issues and Milestones to track coding goals and target dates, keeping the whole subteam aligned in an open, visible format.
Documentation Suite
Dedicated documents are maintained for: control system reference, issues and solutions encountered, autonomous paths and waypoints, robot startup procedures, nightly agenda and progress, and notes from FRC seminars. This keeps every subteam member up to date on rapid developments during the season and enables faster knowledge transfer to new students.
Team Structure & Our Process

We restructured our subteam and adjusted our teaching approach to support a continuously growing group of students, ensuring everyone has a path to meaningful contribution.

Subteam Structure Changes

Pre-Season Group Learning
Before build season, we prioritized group lessons: programming a motor, introduction to command-based Java, programming the ParadeBot ("Spinny Carter"), and programming the XRP Bots (WPILib). Incoming students had more experience at the start of build season and could be more self-sustaining.
Build Season Teaching
Code is visible to all members. Newer students take turns writing and reasoning through code directly, furthering their understanding through doing — not just watching.

PDSA Cycle

Plan
Brainstorm ideas and document them in a weekly-updated goal checklist.
Do
Implement together, following the methods above. Code is visible and collaborative.
Study
Observe how code performed, take notes on improvements and what did or didn't work.
Act
Troubleshoot, fine-tune, and repeat. Results tracked on whiteboards and visible monitors in the shop before being transferred to documentation.
