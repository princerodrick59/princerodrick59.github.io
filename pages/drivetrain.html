Bump Traversal — Rockhopper Iterations

This year, due to us only having the capability to cross the bump, we knew we would need to do a lot with the drivetrain to ensure consistency when crossing the bump.

V1 — Raised Frame (Inverted MK5n)
On kickoff day, one of our first strategic decisions was to invert our MK5n swerve modules, increasing ground clearance from 1.75" to 3.75". This enabled more reliable traversal over the bump while also addressing a key risk we identified early — beaching on loose Fuel scattered across the field. By increasing chassis clearance, we ensured the robot could maintain mobility even when driving over game pieces. However, this introduced a notable tradeoff: increased exposure of drivetrain components. During prototyping, this resulted in impact damage, including the failure of a Kraken x60 motor and two Powerpole adapters.
V2 — Rockhoppers V1 (Welded Stainless)
Inspired by rock-climbing vehicles, our next iteration introduced welded stainless steel "rockhopper" guards designed to form protective ramps around each swerve module, shielding the motor assemblies from impact. These structures were reinforced with internal ribs during the welding process — testing showed minimal deformation and strong overall durability. While highly functional in protecting critical components, the geometry occupied more space on the drivetrain than desired, and the system lacked sufficient rigidity due to not being directly fixed to the drive tubes. As a result, further refinement was required to better balance protection, compactness, and structural integrity.
V3 — Rockhoppers Final (Riveted + Bent Features)
The final iteration builds on V2 with refined geometry to improve consistency when traversing the bump, allowing for smoother contact transitions and more predictable drivetrain behavior over field obstacles. Rigidity was significantly improved by reducing the number of welded joints and incorporating more bent features, leveraging the material properties of the stainless steel to increase stiffness while minimizing added weight. This version is riveted directly into the drive structure, providing a more secure and robust mounting solution.
Hopper Iteration

Also part of the drivetrain is the hopper. Throughout its lifespan it has gone through one major iteration — the addition of flanges and embossments to add rigidity throughout the structure. Flanges were added near the top of each face to increase rigidity through the plate. The front of the hopper was embossed near the bottom to create rigidity in the section that cannot be flanged or affixed to another face.
